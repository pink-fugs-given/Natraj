Every touch softens tension. Every movement dissolves effort. Every pathway leads towards surrender.

Breath deepens. Weight returns.

The urgency disappears.

The performers sit quietly within the space, tracing themselves back into stillness.

The sound remains. The memory remains. The cycle remains.

Gradually, the bodies begin to fold back into themselves.

Natraj

Five bodies are scattered across the space, folded into unfamiliar shapes, compressed into themselves. Their positions feel awkward, unnatural, suspended somewhere between stillness and becoming.

One body in the middle. In their hands rests a Damru.

The first sound cuts through the darkness.

It travels through the space, carving pathways through the air. It is both distant and immediate, ancient and present. The rhythm moves around the room like a current searching for somewhere to settle. It is the sound of creation, but also the sound of emptiness, the sound from which everything emerges.

The bodies begin to respond. A finger circles. A wrist follows.

A shoulder slowly catches the motion.

Natraj

What begins as something almost invisible spreads carefully through the body. A small rotation becomes another. The circles travel like a current beneath the skin, moving from one part of the body to the next. The body unfolds slowly. Limbs lengthen. Spines rise.

Creation is not sudden.

It grows.

The circles become larger, wider, fuller. Momentum gathers. What was once compact begins to expand beyond itself. The performers are carried by the growth of the movement, following circles that become too large to remain contained.

Within this expansion, the double-touch score first appears.

Two hands arrive at the same place.

Natraj

A new pathway opens. The body follows.

The score feels curious here, exploratory. Each touch creates another possibility, another direction, another beginning.

Eventually the sound fades.

The Damru falls silent.

Yet the silence is not empty.

The rhythm remains in the space long after the instrument stops.

The performers continue moving as though preserving its memory.

Repetition emerges. Patterns return. The circles settle into systems.

Natraj

The bodies begin searching for ways to maintain what has been created.

Small sounds appear. Breath. Footsteps. Clicks.

One performer begins connecting the others, moving between bodies, adjusting momentum, calming some, encouraging others. The system slowly starts to organise itself.

The double-touch score remains, but it feels different now.

The touches no longer create.

They sustain. They absorb momentum. They regulate energy. They keep the machine running.

The clicks multiply. One body finds another. Then another. Then another.

Natraj

Like separate parts of a machine gradually discovering how they fit together.

The performers begin walking. Not towards a destination, but towards one another.

Sometimes together. Sometimes apart.

The pathways separate and reconnect endlessly. One performer drifts away. Another changes direction. Someone falls behind. Someone catches up.

Yet they keep finding each other again.

The walking becomes its own choreography of preservation.

An endless attempt to keep something alive.

The system breathes. Expands. Contracts. Reorganises itself.

➞
Natraj

For a while, it works.

Then friction begins to appear. The connections that once held everything together begin to strain. Directions no longer align. One body wants to move forward while another pulls back.

Momentum becomes difficult to maintain. The performers remain connected, yet the connection no longer feels supportive.

It feels resistant. Heavy.

The machine begins to fail. The walking fractures. The pathways collapse.

The bodies pull against one another, caught between opposing desires.

The effort of preservation becomes impossible.

➞
Natraj

Something has to break.

And it does.

Destruction enters.

Movement twists sharply through the body. The shoulders, the head, the hip. They twist slow, they twist fast. Actions become forceful, sudden, relentless, powerful.

The double-touch score returns again.

But now it feels entirely different.

Touches become impacts. Interruptions. Collisions. Every touch carries the possibility of collapse.

Fragments of movement remain.

➞
Natraj

Pieces of phrases repeat and disintegrate.

Twisting bodies unravel the systems that once sustained them. The destruction grows larger and larger until the performers can no longer hold themselves upright.

One by one, they are drawn towards the floor. What was built has broken apart.

And yet something remains.

From the remains emerges illusion.

A body rises. Soft. Weightless. Untouched by what came before. The movement feels effortless, dreamlike, almost impossibly beautiful. Around this softness, traces of destruction still exist, but they are no longer acknowledged.

Attention drifts elsewhere.

➞
Natraj

Awareness narrows.

The world becomes pleasant enough to ignore what lies beneath it.

The double-touch score appears once more.

Now only fingertips are needed. A single finger. Then another. The lightest contact redirects the entire body.

Movement floats.

The performers drift through the space as though gravity has loosened its grip on them. They move through a world that feels suspended between reality and imagination. Slowly, however, another awareness begins to emerge.

The illusion starts to dissolve.

➞
Natraj

The performers begin tracing.

First with their fingertips. Then with their hands. Attention returns to the body. To sensation. To presence.

Release arrives quietly.

The performers settle onto the floor and into themselves. Tracing becomes a form of listening. Fingers travel across skin. Hands explore shoulders, arms, faces, legs. Every touch notices something. Every touch lets something go.

The double-touch score remains. For the final time, it transforms. The touches no longer redirect.

They no longer sustain. They no longer destroy. They release.
